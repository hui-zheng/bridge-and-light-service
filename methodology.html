Methodology

How a piece moves from an English source to a Chinese reader, and what we will not change along the way.

1. Adaptation, not translation

We do not produce literal translations. We produce 再创作: a faithful, culturally native rewrite that lets a Chinese reader meet the original at full register. This means re-pacing for the reader's context, giving brief bridging context for unfamiliar Western references (a name, an institution, a piece of jurisprudence), and rendering the speaker's tone — confidence, frustration, restraint — in a Chinese register that carries the same weight.

What it does not mean: weakening the speaker's argument, sharpening it past where they take it, or adding our own commentary into the body of the piece.

2. Editorial review

Every piece passes through multiple independent review stages before publication. It is read against the source for fidelity — has anything shifted, softened, or sharpened that we cannot defend? It is read for voice — does it carry the speaker's tone, register, and weight in Chinese the way it does in English? It is read again with a native Mandarin reader's ear, to catch the residue of translation that survives the earlier passes. And it is held to the standard of contemporary Chinese essay writing — the difference between Chinese prose that is technically correct and Chinese prose a serious reader would willingly spend time with.

Each stage can send the piece back. A piece does not ship until every stage clears it.

3. Fact-check discipline

We independently verify:

  • Names of people, places, organizations, and institutions.
  • Statistics, dates, and case citations.
  • Quotations attributed to the speaker by other sources.
  • Claims about legislation, court decisions, and public record.

When we cannot independently verify a claim the speaker has made, we soften it (attributing the claim to the speaker rather than presenting it as fact), reframe it as opinion, or, where the claim is unverifiable and not load-bearing, cut it. Once softened, a claim stays softened until we can independently verify it. We keep an internal record of every softening decision and the reason for it, and we disclose this record on request.

We invent nothing. When a Western figure or place has no established Chinese rendering, we keep the English name rather than fabricate one.

4. Source attribution

Every piece links to its primary source — interview, article, video, or transcript — with a publication date and a working URL. This is non-negotiable. Readers and platforms deserve to know exactly where the content came from.

5. What we will not do

  • We do not add our own advocacy or commentary into the body of the piece.
  • We do not strip out the speaker's views to make them more palatable to a Chinese audience.
  • We do not sharpen a claim beyond how the speaker made it.
  • We do not localize anonymously — every piece carries the Bridge and Light editorial line.
  • We do not work on pieces we cannot stand behind.

6. Editorial relationship with the source

Where possible, we share the final Chinese draft with the original speaker or author before publication, alongside a brief in English describing any softenings or bridges we have added. The speaker can correct factual errors and flag any framing they want adjusted. We honor factual corrections. We do not promise editorial control beyond that.
