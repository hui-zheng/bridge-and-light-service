Selected work

A few of our own published pieces, shown as proof of craft.

These are long-form pieces we wrote and published under our own name. They are in Chinese, which is the deliverable and the whole point. You can run any English source through a translation tool in a minute. The trouble is you can't read what comes out, so you have no way to tell whether it is fluent or clumsy, faithful or subtly wrong, before it goes out under your name. The English note under each piece says what it took to get the Chinese right: the reference that had to be made native, the fact a machine would have carried over wrong, the register a diaspora reader trusts. That judgment, in a language you cannot check yourself, is the work.

橱窗里的那块牌子，是怎么挂上去的

On Canada's Bill C-9 and compelled speech, built from MP Tamara Kronis's House of Commons speech and framed through Václav Havel's parable of the greengrocer (The Power of the Powerless).

What it demonstratesA dense religious-freedom and free-speech argument rebuilt so a Chinese reader meets it at full weight, not as a footnote. Havel's greengrocer is a state-shop manager, a small cog, not a private owner, and his whole point rests on that. Reach for the wrong word and the argument quietly collapses, which is exactly what a machine translation does. We checked it against Havel's own text, along with the 1978 essay date, the Keegstra ruling, and the riding names around it.

Source Two House of Commons speeches on Bill C-9: “Can the State ‘Edit the Bible’?” and “The state must not edit the Bible.”

Findlay 律师，与她想关掉的那些诊所

An interview-based profile of Kerry-Lynne Findlay, the lawyer who went on to lead the BC Conservative Party, and the provincial laws she is working to unwind. Researched and published independently, under our own name.

What it demonstratesThe piece where a real politician's name, record, votes, and legal detail all have to be exact. A generic tool, pushed to localize, invents a Chinese name for a figure who has none and carries forward whatever the draft got wrong: an award misidentified, a tenure miscounted, an organization renamed. We refused to invent a Chinese name and used her English one, and corrected roughly a dozen name and record errors before it published. Under a party leader's name, one wrong fact is the whole risk.

Source A BC Conservative leadership-campaign interview: the Findlay-Elston interview.

那栋曾经一份工资就买得起的房子

A federal political-economy argument built from Pierre Poilievre's (博励治) long-form interviews, the Diary of a CEO conversation chief among them: housing, inflation, and energy read as one connected story.

What it demonstratesAn economic argument carried as a single narrative a Chinese reader will both follow and feel, which survives only when it is re-created, not translated. It also shows where errors actually enter: a source gets summarized before anyone translates, and the summary is where a fact slips. One summary had Poilievre growing up in Ottawa; the recording has him in suburban Calgary, "electricians and oil workers on our street." We verify against the original interview, not the summary, so the wrong fact never reaches the reader.

Source Three long-form interviews: Diary of a CEO, Joe Rogan #2470, and Triggernometry.

尼采说“上帝死了”，但几乎没人知道他接下来说了什么

A long-form essay on Nietzsche's "God is dead" and what he said next, built around the atheist historian Tom Holland (Dominion), who set out to show Christianity no longer mattered and wrote himself to the edge of faith.

What it demonstratesThe hardest test of the craft: literary and intellectual register held to the standard of contemporary Chinese essay writing. A machine gets the words right and the voice wrong, and a diaspora reader hears the difference in a single sentence. This is our register benchmark, the piece that answers whether an adaptation reads as native prose or as translation.

Source Tom Holland's Dominion, plus interviews and commentary on its thesis: “Christ and His Endless Resurrections” (The Pull Request), a Free Press interview with Holland, and a Dominion review from The Gospel Coalition.
